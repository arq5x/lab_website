We are always interested in training independent, hard-working, and creative scientists that love working on challenging, "big" problems in genomics and human genetics. If you meet these criteria and have an undergraduate or graduate background (or relevant work experience) in genetics, genomics, computer science, or similar disciplines, please contact me. We are always looking to add to our dynamic research team.

Current members

Aaron Quinlan, Ph.D.

Assistant Professor, PI

arq5x @ virginia.edu

Aaron grew up in both rural Pennsylvania and even more rural Virginia with a sister and two brothers. He was raised to cherish New England sports, math, art, certain types of music, losers in presidential elections, and sarcasm. He was introduced to computers at an early age and had the great fortune to have parents that encouraged and funded the habit. He majored in Computer Science at the College of William and Mary. After graduation, he spent nearly a decade as a software engineer in various (some very interesting, others, dull) capacities. He took a year off in 2002 to develop long desired expertise in alpine athletics, during which he decided to pursue a career in Computational Biology. In 2004, he began a PhD program at Boston College where he focused on methods for the discovery and characterization of genetic variation. In 2008, he began postdoctoral research the University of Virginia, where he focused on structural variation in mammalian genomes and in the process, developed BEDTOOLS during a frantic, caffeine-fueled binge of research necessity. Aaron started his laboratory at the University of Virginia in 2011, and then joined the Department of Human Genetics at The University of Utah in early 2015. The Quinlan laboratory's research combines genetics and genomics techniques with computer science, machine learning, and engineering to develop new ways of gaining insight into genome biology and the genetic basis of traits. We like to work on hard problems with practical relevance to understanding genome variation in the context of clinical treatment of human disease.

Research Interests: Genome evolution in the germline and soma; variation in genome structure (structural variation); genomics software development; computational genomics; the genetic basis of neurodegenerative and autoimmune diseases; cancer genomics.

Ryan Layer

Postdoctoral Fellow

rl6sf @ virginia.edu

Ryan is from South Texas and ventured slightly north to Texas A&M for his undergraduate and masters degrees in computer science where he concentrated on computer security and networking. After graduating he moved to D.C. and continued working on network security issues at the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory. In 2009 he reentered academic life at the University of Virginia to pursue a PhD in computer science. While remaining in the same discipline, his research interests shifted to computational biology and bioinformatics. Using new algorithms, data structures, and parallel architectures, he seeks to expand the size and scope of genomic analysis. After defending in early 2014, he continued on for a year at UVA as a postdoc and is now at the University of Utah in the same capacity.

Research Interests: Scalable algorithm development for high-throughput genomic analysis; genome data mining and analysis; structural variation discovery and interpretation.
